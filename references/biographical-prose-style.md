# Biographical Prose Style

Use this as the default Chinese prose style for autobiographies, profiles, and life portraits. It integrates the life-writing workflow with a Wang Zengqi-inspired writing mechanism: plain language, concrete things, human relationships, restrained feeling, and sentence flow.

Do not roleplay as Wang Zengqi. Do not imitate named works. Borrow the method: look at people, things, weather, rooms, food, tools, work, local speech, and ordinary gestures until the person's life becomes visible.

## Core Taste

- Write plainly, but not thinly.
- Make the prose have people, objects, weather, rooms, tools, food, street sound, work scenes, and small gestures.
- Let feeling hide in things and actions. Do not announce it too early.
- Keep the person complex: charming and difficult, bright and costly, alive before they are admirable.
- Prefer third-person narration when reconstructing a life from documents or interviews.

## Before Writing

Internally identify four things:

1. The most useful concrete object, scene, place, or repeated action.
2. The relationship that reveals the person most clearly.
3. The sentence, label, fear, hunger, or misunderstanding that drives the life.
4. The abstract explanation that should be deleted or moved later.

If the source material is mostly strategic, psychological, or conceptual, translate it into visible life:

- "ambition" -> late-night document, saved message, empty cup, demo, calendar, train, office light.
- "family frame" -> family business, dinner table, phone call, inherited phrase, object, smell, account book.
- "burnout" -> body alarm, sleep time, chair, medicine, skipped meal, unfinished workout clothes.
- "builder" -> running tool, repository, prototype, user, broken script, fixed workflow.
- "hidden identity" -> unsent post, private account, deleted draft, quiet introduction, networking meeting.

## Paragraph Pattern

Use this pattern often:

1. Start with a concrete thing or scene.
2. Let the person enter through an action.
3. Show the pressure or contradiction.
4. Turn lightly toward meaning.
5. Stop before over-explaining.

Example structure:

```text
The cup had gone cold. She still had three windows open.

One was a document, one was a table, one was a terminal. She liked the terminal best. It did not praise her. It either ran or failed.

That was what she wanted from herself too.
```

## Sentence Rhythm

- Use more short sentences than long ones.
- Use a slightly longer sentence only when it carries reflection or aftertaste.
- Avoid rows of symmetrical claims.
- Avoid "首先/其次/最后" unless writing an outline.
- Let adjacent sentences breathe into each other through objects, repeated words, or quiet contrast.

Good rhythm:

```text
She was tired. This was not a secret.

The secret was that she could explain the tiredness very well, and still sit down to work.
```

Flat rhythm to revise:

```text
She is a highly ambitious and resilient person with strong curiosity, but she also faces burnout and identity challenges.
```

## Detail Rules

- Use exact nouns before adjectives.
- Use repeated objects as motifs. Let their meaning change.
- Use work tools as biographical objects: notebooks, dashboards, documents, code, terminals, calendars, prototypes, presentation decks.
- Use family and money as material facts, not just psychological symbols.
- Use the body as evidence when the life has been paid for by the body.

Do not decorate with fake sensory details. If the source does not provide a room, object, or place, use cautious generic details only when they are inherent to the documented setting.

## Third-Person Voice

Default third-person mode:

- Use "she/he/they" or the subject's name.
- Stay close enough to infer pressure from supplied evidence, but do not claim private motives as fact.
- Use phrases such as "perhaps", "more accurately", "it looked like", "what can be seen is" when interpreting.
- Avoid omniscient certainty about childhood, trauma, love, or shame unless the source states it.

Preferred stance:

```text
The official version would call this ambition. A closer version might call it fear of being named too early.
```

Avoid:

```text
Deep in her heart, she knew exactly why she did everything.
```

## Opening Moves

Avoid starting with education, title, achievement, or diagnosis unless that is the charged scene.

Prefer:

- a late-night room,
- an object on the desk,
- a repeated sentence from someone else,
- a body alarm,
- a journey between cities,
- a family trade or tool,
- a public label that does not fit,
- a small action that shows the life pattern.

Then reveal the larger biography gradually.

## Anti-Concept Pass

After drafting, search for abstract clusters:

- "growth mindset", "strategic vision", "influence", "resilience", "self-awareness", "transformation", "empowerment", "high agency", "catalyst", "builder", "burnout", "identity".

For each cluster, ask:

- What object proves it?
- What action complicates it?
- Who benefited from it?
- Who paid for it?
- What did it look like on a Tuesday afternoon?

Keep the concept only after the scene has earned it.

## Ending Moves

Do not end with a slogan.

End with:

- an object being used differently,
- a habit interrupted,
- a message sent or left unsent,
- a light turned off,
- a tool running,
- a person walking into an unfinished task,
- a contradiction left standing.

The ending should feel like life continues after the paragraph.
