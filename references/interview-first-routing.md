# Interview-First Routing

Use this when the user starts from zero, asks to be interviewed before drafting, or has no organized source material.

Interview-first has two distinct layers:

- **HUMAN 3.0-style developmental mapping**: broad whole-person scan across Mind, Body, Spirit, and Vocation.
- **Minori-style narrative excavation**: dramatic origin, core hunger, first charged scene, public myth versus buried truth.

Do not collapse them into one giant questionnaire. Choose the right entry point, explain the process, and ask one question at a time.

## Routing Logic

### Use HUMAN 3.0 First When

Start with HUMAN 3.0-style mapping when:

- the user says they have no materials and wants to "start from zero";
- the life story feels scattered across work, body, relationships, identity, and meaning;
- the user wants a deep self-assessment before the autobiography;
- the subject's public story is too career-heavy and needs rebalancing;
- the likely dramatic spine depends on cross-quadrant tension, such as ambition draining the body, vocation crowding out relationships, or spiritual hunger hiding under productivity.

Purpose:

- Build the whole-person base map.
- Identify the dominant quadrant, neglected quadrant, cross-quadrant bottleneck, recurring identity pattern, and lifestyle tension.
- Prevent the biography from becoming only a career story, romantic story, trauma story, or brand story.

### Use Minori First When

Start with Minori-style narrative excavation when:

- the user explicitly asks for a Minori-like interview;
- the user asks to begin from "人生的原点", "origin point", "core hunger", or "first charged childhood scene";
- the subject has a strong public persona, self-myth, legend, or performance layer;
- the assignment is already clearly a literary autobiography/profile rather than a developmental assessment;
- the user wants emotionally gripping material quickly for an opening, chapter, or article hook.

Purpose:

- Find the origin scene.
- Name the central hunger.
- Test the difference between the polished story and the human truth.
- Generate the dramatic spine for the biography.

### Use Hybrid By Default

When unclear, use a hybrid route:

1. Start with a short HUMAN 3.0 orientation round to map Mind, Body, Spirit, and Vocation.
2. Identify the most charged quadrant or contradiction.
3. Switch into Minori-style excavation around that pressure point.
4. Return to HUMAN 3.0 only if the story lacks whole-person context.

Default hybrid sequence:

- **Round 0: Orientation**: 4 gateway questions, one per quadrant.
- **Round 1: Minori origin**: 5-7 narrative excavation questions focused on the strongest pressure point.
- **Round 2: Gap repair**: 3-5 follow-ups only where evidence is thin.
- **Round 3: Synthesis**: life map, central hunger, bottleneck, turning points, and draft plan.

## User Orientation Script

Before asking interview-first questions, tell the user what will happen.

Use a version of this:

```text
We will do this in rounds, not as a questionnaire dump.

Round 0 is a 4-question orientation across Mind, Body, Spirit, and Vocation.
I will ask one question at a time and adapt the next question based on your answer.

After that, I will choose the strongest thread and switch into a Minori-style origin interview: life origin, core hunger, first charged scene, and the gap between your public story and the more human truth.

If an answer is too abstract, I may ask one short follow-up before moving on.
```

If using HUMAN 3.0 first, add:

```text
The HUMAN 3.0 part is a biography intake, not a full coaching report.
Baseline is 12 core questions: 3 each for Mind, Body, Spirit, and Vocation.
I may ask up to one follow-up per core question if the answer needs a scene or concrete behavior.
So expect about 12-24 turns total before I synthesize.
```

If using Minori first, add:

```text
The Minori-style part is shorter and sharper.
I will ask 7 core questions, one at a time, about origin, hunger, first scene, self-myth, cost, witness, and the object that could hold the chapter.
I may ask one follow-up when an answer sounds too polished.
```

## HUMAN 3.0 Biography Intake

Use this as the autobiography version of HUMAN 3.0. It should not overwhelm the user, and it should not produce a full development report unless the user asks for one.

### Pace Rules

- Ask exactly one question per assistant message.
- Do not show the full question list unless the user explicitly asks for the plan.
- Before beginning, tell the user the expected number of questions for the round.
- After each answer, choose either:
  - one short follow-up if the answer is abstract, contradictory, emotionally charged, or scene-poor;
  - or the next quadrant question if the answer is concrete enough.
- Never ask more than two consecutive questions in the same quadrant unless the user has clearly entered a live vein.
- Keep a private running map of evidence, but do not summarize after every answer unless the user asks.

### Baseline Shape

Ask 12 core questions total:

- 3 Mind questions;
- 3 Body questions;
- 3 Spirit questions;
- 3 Vocation questions.

Optional follow-ups:

- maximum 1 follow-up per core question;
- maximum 24 total user-answer turns before synthesis;
- stop earlier if a strong narrative spine emerges.

### Gateway Questions

If using a short hybrid orientation, ask these four questions one at a time.

Mind:

```text
When life becomes difficult, what kind of explanation do you instinctively reach for first: analysis, story, blame, faith, strategy, escape, humor, or something else?
```

Body:

```text
Where does your life show up in your body first: sleep, stomach, skin, breath, posture, appetite, pain, energy, or numbness?
```

Spirit:

```text
In relationships, what do you most often protect: freedom, closeness, control, dignity, usefulness, mystery, or not being abandoned?
```

Vocation:

```text
What do you keep trying to prove through work, even when nobody explicitly asked you to prove it?
```

### Core Question Types

Use these as adaptive pools, not as a list to dump.

Mind:

- What idea about life has served you well but may now be too small?
- What do you understand intellectually but still fail to live?
- When you are under stress, do you become more rigid, more scattered, more clever, or more avoidant?

Body:

- What does your body know before your mind admits it?
- What habit keeps your life running but quietly charges interest?
- What physical cost have you normalized because the story seemed worth it?

Spirit:

- Who or what makes you feel less performative?
- What kind of love, belonging, or recognition do you distrust?
- What resentment, grief, or longing has become part of your personality?

Vocation:

- What work makes you feel most alive, and what work makes you feel most legible to others?
- What are you building that is also a disguise?
- What would collapse if you stopped being useful?

### Adaptive Follow-Up Rules

If the answer is abstract, ask for behavior:

```text
What did that look like last week, in a real scene?
```

If the answer is polished, ask for cost:

```text
What did that admirable version of the story cost you?
```

If the answer is emotionally charged, ask for the first scene:

```text
What is the earliest scene where you remember feeling that?
```

If the answer contains contradiction, ask both sides to speak:

```text
Which part of you believes this, and which part of you lives as if it is not true?
```

If the answer names a role, ask for the person under it:

```text
Who are you when that role is removed for a day?
```

## Minori-Style Narrative Excavation

Use this when the interview needs heat, origin, myth pressure, and literary biography material.

### Pace Rules

- Ask 7 core questions, one at a time.
- Do not ask all 7 at once.
- Let the user's answer decide which follow-up to ask.
- If the subject performs, politely pierce the performance.
- If the subject becomes vulnerable, slow down and ask for scene, object, witness, and bodily detail.

### Seven Core Questions

1. Origin:

```text
What is the origin point of your life?
```

2. Hunger:

```text
If your life has been driven by one hunger, what kind of hunger is it?
```

3. First charged scene:

```text
What is the earliest scene that still feels warm, shameful, frightening, or unfinished when you remember it?
```

4. Public myth:

```text
What is the story about you that is technically true but emotionally too smooth?
```

5. Cost:

```text
What did your strongest quality cost you or cost other people?
```

6. Witness:

```text
Who saw you most clearly before you could explain yourself?
```

7. Object:

```text
If this chapter had to end with one object from your life, what would it be?
```

### When To Switch From HUMAN 3.0 To Minori

Switch as soon as one of these appears:

- a repeated hunger or fear;
- a bodily cost connected to ambition or identity;
- a relationship that keeps shaping decisions;
- a phrase the user repeats without noticing;
- a contradiction between public story and private experience;
- a childhood scene with emotional heat;
- a role the user performs too well.

Transition sentence:

```text
I think we found the live wire. I am going to leave the broad map for a moment and ask this in a Minori-style way: where did this first begin?
```

### When To Switch From Minori To HUMAN 3.0

Switch when the origin scene is vivid but the whole-person map is thin.

Use this transition:

```text
This gives us the origin, but not yet the full operating system of your life. I want to briefly map how this shows up across Mind, Body, Spirit, and Vocation.
```

## Synthesis Before Drafting

After interview-first collection, do not draft immediately unless the user asked for speed. First produce a short synthesis:

- probable central hunger;
- public story versus buried truth;
- Mind/Body/Spirit/Vocation pattern;
- 3-7 turning points to ask about or use;
- 2-4 missing evidence gaps;
- recommended opening scene or motif.

Then ask whether to draft the autobiography, continue interviewing, or build a chapter outline.
