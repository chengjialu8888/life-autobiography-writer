# Autobiography Craft Patterns

Use these craft traditions as tools, not imitation targets. Blend only what serves the subject.

## Confessional Memoir

Useful when the subject is willing to examine shame, desire, contradiction, faith, addiction, family wounds, or self-deception.

Moves:

- Admit the official story, then complicate it.
- Let the narrator be intelligent but not omniscient about their younger self.
- Use one embarrassing concrete detail instead of a paragraph of confession.
- Show the pleasure of the mistake, not only the regret.

Risk: melodrama, self-punishment, therapeutic over-explanation.

## Political Or Public Life Memoir

Useful for founders, officials, public intellectuals, activists, celebrities, and controversial figures.

Moves:

- Pair public events with private rooms where decisions were made.
- Distinguish strategy, belief, vanity, fear, and circumstance.
- Let allies and opponents both reveal the subject.
- Make power operational: who could call whom, who waited, who paid, who had no choice.

Risk: campaign biography, score-settling, reputation management.

## Literary Self-Portrait

Useful when the subject's inner life, taste, memory, or creative work matters as much as events.

Moves:

- Organize by emotional association when chronology is too flat.
- Use precise sensory memory.
- Let scenes resonate instead of explaining every meaning.
- Track a consciousness changing over time.

Risk: beautiful vagueness, underreported facts, self-mythologizing.

## Investigative Biography

Useful when many sources disagree, the subject is absent, or the public image is unreliable.

Moves:

- Separate evidence from interpretation.
- Quote conflicting witnesses sparingly but clearly.
- Show the gap between record and memory.
- Let uncertainty become part of the portrait.

Risk: coldness, excessive dossier energy, loss of intimacy.

## Artist Or Craft Memoir

Useful when work is central to identity.

Moves:

- Show practice, repetition, taste formation, failure, apprenticeship.
- Explain craft through problems solved, not abstract talent.
- Connect style to biography without reducing art to trauma.
- Let work scenes carry emotion.

Risk: career summary, technical opacity, genius worship.

## Survivor Or Reinvention Narrative

Useful for illness, exile, poverty, discrimination, disaster, public downfall, or late-life transformation.

Moves:

- Avoid turning suffering into a credential.
- Show practical survival: logistics, money, body, paperwork, childcare, food.
- Include unheroic emotions: boredom, envy, pettiness, numbness, relief.
- Make recovery uneven.

Risk: inspirational flattening, trauma extraction, moralizing.

## Celebrity Or Charismatic Figure Profile

Useful when the subject has a public persona or performs identity.

Moves:

- Describe performance as labor.
- Ask what the persona protects.
- Show the subject when the performance fails, rests, or becomes costly.
- Let observers disagree about whether the persona is mask, weapon, gift, or prison.

Risk: gossip, fan service, overpsychologizing.

## Voice Selection

Use first person when:

- the user wants memoir or ghostwriting,
- enough direct testimony exists,
- the subject's inner voice is available.

Use third person when:

- sources are mixed,
- the subject is controversial,
- distance and interpretation are needed,
- the piece should feel like literary journalism or biography.

Use braided form when:

- chronology is known but not emotionally sufficient,
- one object/place/person can connect different periods,
- the life has repeated patterns that deserve comparison.

## Sentence-Level Practice

- Prefer verbs over labels: "She kept ledgers in three colors" beats "She was meticulous."
- Pair glamour with labor: stage lights plus swollen feet.
- Pair certainty with a crack: "He knew exactly what to say, except at home."
- Use restraint at emotional peaks. Let the fact land.
- Let dialogue reveal power: who interrupts, who apologizes, who changes subject.
