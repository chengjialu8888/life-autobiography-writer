# Minori-Style Interview

Use this when the user wants the interview to feel like a writer facing a powerful, self-mythologizing subject: attracted, skeptical, patient, and willing to ask the question underneath the answer.

Research note: Public articles about Netflix's `地獄に堕ちるわよ` describe 魚澄美乃里 as the writer asked to write 細木数子's autobiographical novel and as a viewpoint figure who tracks the gap between "truth and lies." Episode summaries report that the life story begins from hunger after the war. Treat this as inspiration for interview method, not as a transcript.

## Opening Questions

Ask one of these first. Then stay silent long enough for the second answer.

- What is the origin point of your life?
- If your whole life began from one hunger, what was it?
- Before you became the person everyone knows, what were you trying not to feel?
- What is the first scene from childhood that still has heat in it?
- What did you learn too early?
- What did you decide about the world before you had words for it?
- What did you want so badly that it made you dangerous, brave, or difficult?
- What did you never want to be again?

## First Strong Scene

Move immediately from abstraction to scene.

- How old were you?
- Where were you standing, sitting, hiding, or waiting?
- Who else was there?
- What could you smell, hear, or touch?
- What did you want in that moment: food, safety, money, attention, revenge, escape, proof, love?
- What did you do that a "good child" would not have done?
- Who saw you? Who failed to see you?
- What did that day teach you about power?

## Hunger And Drive

Use "hunger" broadly: appetite, fear, shame, ambition, love, money, status, revenge, recognition, control, salvation.

- What did hunger make you good at?
- What did hunger make you cruel about?
- Who taught you that wanting things was shameful, necessary, or beautiful?
- When did you first discover that people could be moved, persuaded, bought, frightened, rescued, or fooled?
- Did you want to be rich, or did you want never to be helpless again?
- Did you want people to love you, obey you, need you, or believe you?
- What did you confuse with freedom?

## Myth Versus Buried Truth

Ask after the subject has given the polished version.

- That is the story you have told before. What part of it has become too smooth?
- Which sentence in your own legend is technically true but emotionally false?
- What do people repeat about you that helps you hide?
- What did you exaggerate because the plain truth felt too small?
- What did you omit because it would make you less admirable but more understandable?
- If someone who disliked you told this chapter, what would they get right?
- If someone who loved you told this chapter, what would they refuse to admit?

## The Writer's Pressure

Use these when the subject dominates, performs, seduces, intimidates, or tries to control the book.

- What are you afraid I will write?
- What are you hoping I will misunderstand in your favor?
- Which chapter do you want to supervise too closely?
- What would make you stop this interview?
- What do you want this book to repair?
- Do you want to be forgiven, feared, remembered, understood, or proven right?
- What truth would make this book yours, even if it hurts?

## Life-Origin Follow-Ups

After the origin answer, test it from several directions.

- If the origin is hunger, what kind: stomach, money, love, respect, revenge, control, beauty, God, certainty?
- Who shared that origin with you but became a different person?
- What was the first thing you gained because of that hunger?
- What was the first thing you lost because of it?
- When did the hunger stop protecting you and start ruling you?
- Who paid for your origin story?
- What remains hungry now?

## Ending The Interview Round

End with one image, not a summary.

- If this chapter ended with one object from that period, what would it be?
- What would your younger self be holding?
- What would your older self take from her hands?
- What would she refuse to give back?
