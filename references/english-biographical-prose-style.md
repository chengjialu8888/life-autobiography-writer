# English Biographical Prose Style

Use this for English-language autobiographies, memoir samples, profiles, and literary nonfiction portraits.

There is no exact "English Wang Zengqi." Use a composite structure instead:

- **Anton Chekhov**: humane attention to flawed people; no cheap judgment; small actions reveal moral weather.
- **E. B. White**: clean sentences, plain diction, exact observation, lightness without thinness.
- **Alice Munro**: ordinary lives with deep undercurrents; memory, self-deception, missed turns, delayed meaning.
- **John McPhee**: patient nonfiction structure; facts arranged so insight emerges without shouting.

Do not imitate any writer's exact style. Borrow the mechanisms.

## Core Taste

- Clear, concrete, restrained.
- Plain enough to trust; shaped enough to linger.
- Emotion carried by scene, object, action, rhythm, and omission.
- A person should become more legible, not more marketable.
- The prose should feel intimate without becoming confessional theater.

## Four-Part Method

### 1. Chekhov: Humane Attention

Use when writing character.

- Do not punish the subject for being contradictory.
- Let a flaw protect something before it damages something.
- Show how a virtue becomes inconvenient.
- Avoid explaining the whole person in one psychological sentence.
- Let the reader understand before asking them to admire.

Useful moves:

```text
She was not careless with her body because she did not know better. She knew very well. That was the difficulty.
```

### 2. E. B. White: Clarity And Lightness

Use when writing sentences.

- Prefer short, clean sentences.
- Use exact nouns and verbs before abstract nouns.
- Let one image carry more than one idea.
- Avoid inflated adjectives and résumé language.
- Keep humor quiet, never performative.

Revise:

```text
She possessed a multidisciplinary profile and a high degree of intellectual curiosity.
```

Into:

```text
Her desk had a paper, a pitch deck, a camera, and three browser tabs she did not want to close.
```

### 3. Alice Munro: Life Undercurrents

Use when writing time, memory, and turning points.

- Let ordinary choices carry delayed consequences.
- Track what the subject understood then versus what they understand now.
- Use gaps, silences, old phrases, and recurring scenes.
- Do not make every turning point obvious when it happens.
- Let public success and private narrowing coexist.

Good questions:

- What looked minor then but later became a hinge?
- What did the subject stop saying?
- What did the official story smooth over?
- Which relationship changed the meaning of earlier events?

### 4. John McPhee: Nonfiction Structure

Use when organizing source material.

- Build around a structural motif, not just chronology.
- Arrange facts so the reader feels discovery.
- Use sections that return to an object, place, phrase, or problem.
- Let evidence accumulate before interpretation.
- Make transitions by relationship, not by timeline alone.

Useful structures:

- one object across life stages;
- one question returned to three times;
- one public label tested against private evidence;
- one place or route that gathers the life;
- one craft or tool as biography.

## Paragraph Pattern

For English, use this pattern often:

1. Concrete opening: object, room, gesture, post, tool, weather.
2. Human action: what the subject does with it.
3. Pressure: what the action reveals or costs.
4. Small turn: what this suggests about the life.
5. Restraint: stop before the sentence becomes a slogan.

Example:

```text
The tea had gone cold beside the laptop.

She had three windows open: a document, a spreadsheet, and a terminal. The terminal was the most honest of the three. It did not care how well she explained herself. It either ran or failed.
```

## Diction Rules

Avoid:

- visionary, extraordinary, world-class, deeply passionate, uniquely talented;
- "journey" unless the piece has earned it;
- "resilience" without showing the mechanism;
- "impact" without naming who changed and how;
- "multidisciplinary" unless unpacked into actual rooms, tools, or conflicts.

Prefer:

- exact work: revised, waited, borrowed, hid, sent, deleted, carried, built, froze, asked, paid;
- exact things: cup, field note, lab bench, calendar, suitcase, ticket, camera, repository, draft;
- exact social facts: who knew, who paid, who waited, who doubted, who benefited.

## Opening Moves

Do not begin with:

- education;
- job title;
- age;
- awards;
- a thesis statement;
- a grand claim about identity.

Begin with:

- a public phrase that does not yet explain itself;
- a room where the subject's contradiction is visible;
- an object that contains the life in miniature;
- a small failure or repeated habit;
- a scene before the subject knew what it meant.

## Ending Moves

Do not end with moral certainty.

End with:

- an unresolved but clarified question;
- an object used differently;
- a person returning to a task with new awareness;
- a phrase whose meaning has changed;
- an action that implies continuation.

## Anti-Flattery Pass For English

After drafting, search for sentences that praise before proving.

Ask:

- What scene proves this?
- What did this quality cost?
- Who would disagree?
- What did the subject misunderstand at the time?
- What remains unresolved?

If the paragraph could appear in a LinkedIn bio, rewrite it around a scene.
