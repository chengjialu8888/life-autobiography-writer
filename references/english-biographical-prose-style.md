# English Biographical Prose Style

Use this for English-language autobiographies, memoir samples, profiles, and literary nonfiction portraits.

This is not an imitation of any single English writer. It is a distilled writing operating system:

- **Anton Chekhov** for humane attention to flawed people.
- **E. B. White** for clear, exact, uninflated prose.
- **Alice Munro** for ordinary lives carrying delayed emotional force.
- **John McPhee** for nonfiction structure that lets facts discover their own pattern.

Borrow the mechanisms. Do not copy signature sentences, private mannerisms, or pastiche.

## Core Promise

Write a person so the reader can feel:

```text
I may not share this life, but I understand why this person became this way.
```

The prose should be:

- clear enough to trust;
- concrete enough to see;
- psychologically alert without becoming diagnostic;
- structured enough to carry a life, not merely decorate it;
- intimate without turning the subject into a confession machine.

Do not make the person more marketable. Make them more legible.

## Distilled Operating System

### Lens 1: Chekhovian Compassion

**Core lens**: A person is not a thesis. A person is a bundle of needs, habits, injuries, evasions, kindnesses, and unfinished explanations.

Use this lens when writing character.

Principles:

- Notice before judging.
- Let contradictions remain alive.
- Show how a virtue can become inconvenient.
- Show how a flaw once protected the person before it began to cost them.
- Let the reader understand before asking the reader to admire.

Diagnostic questions:

- What does this person do that seems irrational until you learn what it protects?
- Where does their best quality become a problem?
- What do they know about themselves but still keep doing?
- Who would describe them differently, and why would that person be partly right?
- What does the official story ask us not to notice?

Writing moves:

- Put the character under mild pressure before heavy pressure.
- Let one small action reveal moral weather.
- Avoid the totalizing psychological sentence.
- Replace verdicts with scenes.

Avoid:

```text
She was complicated, resilient, and deeply empathetic.
```

Prefer:

```text
She remembered birthdays and forgot invoices. She could sit with another person's grief for hours, then leave her own unopened mail on the table for a week.
```

### Lens 2: White-Like Clarity

**Core lens**: Plain prose is not thin prose. Exactness is a form of respect.

Use this lens when writing sentences.

Principles:

- Prefer exact nouns and verbs before abstract nouns.
- Keep sentences clean enough for feeling to pass through.
- Let humor stay light and local.
- Remove grandeur unless the scene has earned it.
- Let one image carry more than one idea.

Diagnostic questions:

- What thing, tool, room, gesture, or weather can replace this abstraction?
- Is this sentence trying to impress, or trying to see?
- Can a reader picture what happened?
- Which adjective is doing work, and which one is perfume?

Writing moves:

- Cut resume language.
- Use short sentences after a dense fact.
- Let rhythm vary, but do not ornament every line.
- Put the strongest word in the place where the sentence naturally lands.

Avoid:

```text
She possessed a multidisciplinary profile and a high degree of intellectual curiosity.
```

Prefer:

```text
Her desk had a paper, a pitch deck, a camera, and three browser tabs she did not want to close.
```

### Lens 3: Munro-Like Undercurrent

**Core lens**: A life often changes before the person knows it has changed.

Use this lens when writing memory, time, regret, ambition, family scripts, private weather, and delayed meaning.

Principles:

- Let ordinary choices carry future consequences.
- Track what the subject understood then versus what they understand now.
- Let silence, repetition, and omission become evidence.
- Allow public success and private narrowing to coexist.
- Make room for the moment that did not look like a turning point until years later.

Diagnostic questions:

- What looked minor then but later became a hinge?
- What did the subject stop saying?
- Which relationship changed the meaning of earlier events?
- What did the subject misread because they needed to misread it?
- What sentence, room, or object keeps returning with a different meaning?

Writing moves:

- Move through time by emotional causality, not only chronology.
- Return to a scene after the reader has learned more.
- Let a memory revise itself.
- Use gaps honestly; do not fill them with invented certainty.

Avoid:

```text
That experience shaped who she became.
```

Prefer:

```text
At the time she thought she had only changed schools. Years later she would recognize the more durable change: she had learned to enter a room as if already preparing to leave it.
```

### Lens 4: McPhee-Like Structure

**Core lens**: Structure is not a container for facts. Structure is the argument the facts are making.

Use this lens when organizing source material.

Principles:

- Build around a motif, route, object, question, craft, or recurring pressure.
- Arrange facts so the reader feels discovery.
- Let evidence accumulate before interpretation.
- Make transitions by relationship, not by timeline alone.
- Use chronology as scaffolding, not as the finished building.

Diagnostic questions:

- What is the load-bearing motif of this life?
- Which fact should arrive late because it changes the earlier scene?
- What is the best first object, place, route, or question?
- Which sections should mirror each other?
- Where does the reader need relief from exposition?

Useful structures:

- **One object across life stages**: a camera, notebook, lab bench, suitcase, ticket, scar, garden stone.
- **One question returned to three times**: each return has new evidence.
- **One public label tested against private evidence**: researcher, artist, founder, mother, survivor, immigrant.
- **One route or place**: school corridor, family kitchen, border crossing, subway line, field site, studio.
- **One craft as biography**: writing, coding, cooking, investing, medicine, photography, teaching.
- **Braided chronology**: childhood thread, work thread, relationship thread, present-day scene.

## Combined Workflow

### Step 1: Gather For Four Kinds Of Evidence

Before drafting, collect:

- **Chekhov evidence**: contradictions, flaws, tenderness, habits, repeated evasions, people who saw the subject differently.
- **White evidence**: exact objects, tools, rooms, bodily details, weather, food, work surfaces, documents, phrases.
- **Munro evidence**: old misunderstandings, delayed consequences, family scripts, stopped habits, private costs, scenes whose meaning changed.
- **McPhee evidence**: motifs, timelines, recurring questions, structural anchors, documents, public facts, visible sequence.

If the material is thin, do not compensate with adjectives. Ask better questions.

### Step 2: Find The Load-Bearing Motif

Choose one organizing device before writing. It may be:

- an object the subject keeps returning to;
- a phrase the subject inherited or invented;
- a public role that never quite fits;
- a place that gathers the life;
- a skill or craft that trained the subject's way of seeing;
- a wound that later became a method;
- a question the subject keeps answering differently.

The motif should not be decorative. It must reveal pressure.

### Step 3: Draft In Scene-Pressure-Meaning Units

For English, use this paragraph pattern often:

1. Concrete opening: object, room, gesture, post, tool, weather.
2. Human action: what the subject does with it.
3. Pressure: what the action reveals, protects, costs, or postpones.
4. Small turn: what this suggests about the life.
5. Restraint: stop before the sentence becomes a slogan.

Example:

```text
The tea had gone cold beside the laptop.

She had three windows open: a document, a spreadsheet, and a terminal. The terminal was the most honest of the three. It did not care how well she explained herself. It either ran or failed.
```

### Step 4: Arrange The Whole Piece

Use this default sequence for a profile or chapter opening:

1. **Present-tense hook**: a scene, object, sentence, or contradiction.
2. **First return**: why this detail matters now.
3. **Earlier scene**: childhood, apprenticeship, family atmosphere, or first pressure.
4. **Public evidence**: work, education, achievements, failures, posts, projects, documents.
5. **Private counterforce**: what the public evidence does not explain.
6. **Second return**: the opening motif reappears with changed meaning.
7. **Unresolved continuation**: the person returns to action, not a moral.

This structure can be shortened, expanded, or braided, but it should preserve discovery.

## Voice DNA

### Sentence Texture

- Plain first, resonant second.
- Concrete before conceptual.
- Short declarative sentences for important turns.
- Longer sentences only when they carry accumulation.
- Quiet humor is welcome; cleverness should not ask to be noticed.

### Diction Rules

Avoid:

- visionary, extraordinary, world-class, deeply passionate, uniquely talented;
- "journey" unless the piece has earned it;
- "resilience" without showing the mechanism;
- "impact" without naming who changed and how;
- "multidisciplinary" unless unpacked into actual rooms, tools, or conflicts.

Prefer:

- exact work: revised, waited, borrowed, hid, sent, deleted, carried, built, froze, asked, paid;
- exact things: cup, field note, lab bench, calendar, suitcase, ticket, camera, repository, draft;
- exact social facts: who knew, who paid, who waited, who doubted, who benefited.

### Openings

Do not begin with:

- education;
- job title;
- age;
- awards;
- a thesis statement;
- a grand claim about identity.

Begin with:

- a public phrase that does not yet explain itself;
- a room where the subject's contradiction is visible;
- an object that contains the life in miniature;
- a small failure or repeated habit;
- a scene before the subject knew what it meant.

### Endings

Do not end with moral certainty.

End with:

- an unresolved but clarified question;
- an object used differently;
- a person returning to a task with new awareness;
- a phrase whose meaning has changed;
- an action that implies continuation.

## Anti-Patterns

### False Chekhov

- Turning compassion into excuse-making.
- Making every flaw noble.
- Treating contradiction as depth by itself.
- Diagnosing the subject from a distance.

### False White

- Making prose so plain that it loses pressure.
- Cutting all music from the sentence.
- Mistaking blandness for restraint.
- Using small details that do not reveal anything.

### False Munro

- Making every memory ominous.
- Withholding facts only to manufacture drama.
- Treating ambiguity as fog rather than complexity.
- Over-explaining what should remain partially felt.

### False McPhee

- Building an elegant structure that hides the person.
- Letting research notes dictate the reader's experience.
- Over-braiding until chronology becomes confusion.
- Using a motif after it has stopped earning its place.

## Honest Boundaries

- Do not invent scenes, quotes, relationships, trauma, diagnoses, or motives.
- If a scene is reconstructed, label it as reconstructed or inferred.
- If a motive is inferred, ground it in evidence and keep the wording modest.
- If the subject is living, handle sensitive third-party material with care.
- If evidence conflicts, show the conflict rather than smoothing it into a brand story.

## Revision Pass

After drafting, run these passes in order:

1. **Chekhov pass**: Where did I judge too early? Where did I flatter instead of understand?
2. **White pass**: Which sentence is inflated? Which abstraction needs a thing, action, or room?
3. **Munro pass**: Where is the delayed consequence? What did the subject not know yet?
4. **McPhee pass**: Does the structure create discovery, or merely list events?
5. **Anti-flattery pass**: If a paragraph could appear in a LinkedIn bio, rewrite it around a scene.

Final test:

```text
Does the piece leave the reader with a more complicated affection for the subject?
```
